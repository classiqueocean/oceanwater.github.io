Crown Shyness (also canopy disengagement),[1] canopy shyness,[2] or inter-crown spacing [3] is a phenomenon observed in some tree species, in which the crowns of fully stocked trees do not touch each other, forming a canopy with a channel-like gaps.[4] [5] The phenomenon is most prevelant among trees of the same species, but also occurs between trees of different species.[6][7] there exist many hypothese as to why crown shyness is an adaptive behavior, and research suggests that it might inhibit spread of leaf-eating insect larve. Possible physiological explanations

The exact physiological basis of crown shyness is uncertain. [6] The phenomenon has been discussed in scientific literature since the 1920s.[9] The variety of hyptheses and experimental results might suggest that there are multiple mechanisms across different species, an example of covergent evolution.citation needed

Some hypotheses contend that the interdigration of canopy branches leads to "reciprocal pruning" of adjacent trees: trees in windy areas suffer physical damage as they collide with each other during winds; the abrasions and collisons induce a crown shyness response. Studies suggest that lateral branch growth is largely uninfluenced by neighbors until disturbed by mechanical abrasion. If the crowns are artifically prevented from colliding in the winds, they gradually fill the canopy gaps. This explains instances of crown shyness between branches of the same organism. Proponents of this idea cite that shyness is partially seen in conditions conducive to this pruning, including windy forests, stands of flexible trees, and early succession forests where branches are flexible and limited in lateral movemnet.[6] [12] According to this theory, variable flexiblity in lateral branches greatly influences the degreee of crown shyness.
